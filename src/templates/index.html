Rangers Farm System

Tracking the rebuild

The Texas Rangers are rebuilding in earnest. A look at the next wave of talent that the club hopes will lead it back to glory.

By Evan Grant | Staff Writer

Published April 6, 2019

Updated

    The Rangers’ organization topped all of baseball in minor league win percentage in 2019.

    If only that told the whole story.

    It’s not that winning doesn’t matter in the minors. It’s just that it doesn’t matter much. Player development, ultimately, is about individual players. The teams change dramatically over the course of the year based on individual performances (not to mention injuries), so winning becomes secondary to moving players along. Ideally, the best players move together as a group, but that’s just not how it works.

    So, take the .567 winning percentage with a grain of salt. Consider that Baseball America, which ranks organizational talent, had the Rangers at No. 27 of 30 organizations in its most recent update. And that was up from 28th. The only teams below them are clubs that you would consider in win-now mode – the Cubs, Mets and Milwaukee. The Rangers are admittedly restocking the farm system.

    There is work to be done. The Rangers appear to be overhauling their player development system but moving first-year director Matt Blood out of the role, dismissing the entire Triple-A staff and Double-A manager Joe Mikulik. And there may be more changes to come.

    It was also a frustrating year on the injury front with multiple season-ending injuries for top-tier pitchers. But there were good stories, too.

    Below are our minor league award winners (not to be confused with the official award winners the Rangers will announce after the season and present in January):

    Player of the Year: 1B Curtis Terry. If this was for the prospect of the year, it would go to Sam Huff, who may well win the organization’s award. Excluding journeymen Matt Davidson and Patrick Wilso, who both have major league experience, Huff led the organization’s prospects in homers (28). Here, though, he’s the runner up to Terry, a 13th round pick in 2015. Playing at Class A and advanced Class A, as Huff did, Terry had 25 homers and bettered Huff in RBIs (80) and across the board in slash line: .293/.362/.537/.899. Terry is 22, a year older than Huff and maybe a touch old for Class A, but he’s broken through this year.

    Starting Pitcher of the Year: RHP Jason Bahr. The Rangers essentially bought Bahr, a 5th round pick in 2017, from San Francisco last year, taking on more than $4 million owed to Austin Jackson to get him. In a season split between advanced Class A and Double-A, Bahr went 10-4 with a 2.51 ERA and maintained a strikeout-per-inning average (126 in 122 innings). His WHIP of 1.074 ranked 19th in all of the minors and was second in the system to Class A starter Tim Brennan. Brennan or hot Ricky Vanasco (more on him in the Top 10) would probably be our runnerup.

    Reliever of the Year: RHP DeMarcus Evans. Evans might have had the best season of any prospect in the organization. The only reason he doesn’t crack our top 10 is because of how relief prospects are generally valued. About to turn 23, Evans, a 25th round pick in 2015, will be placed on the 40-man roster this fall after posting incredibly gaudy stats: a 0.90 ERA in 60 innings split between advanced Class A and Double-A, 100 strikeouts and a .119 opponents batting average. The only thing that kept him from a callup in September might be that he walked 22 in 37 1/3 innings after being promoted to Frisco. The solidly built power arm will almost certainly make his major league debut in 2020.
